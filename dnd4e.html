D&D 4th Edition

Unquestionably D&D's most controversial edition, D&D4e's reception was soured by changes to the Open Game License around the time of its release, allowing Wizards of the Coast, its publisher, to collect more money from content created off of their first new TTRPG. Ironically, this effort left the system far less profitable than it could have been, and socially derelicted.

D&D 4e has a great rising community, as more people actually play it and challenge the public perception, which often fails to even be grounded in fact.

Homebrew

D&D4e's homebrew community never got to really spread its wings due to the TableTop Gaming community's rejection of the system, and much of the homebrew which is emerging is from new 4th Edition renaissances. This lack of homebrew definitely hurts D&D4e, though, as the system could do well with a character- building tool; The system relies heavily on combing through splatbooks to find features and powers for the class you're playing to put together your build, and a single site which highlights all of those options in a single managable format would greatly benefit new and old players.

Meanwhile, a lot of 5th edition's modularity originally came from D&D4e, and was then scuttled to make the next edition by casting aside the name that failed. This similarity to D&D5e is also the reason for much of D&D4e's renaissance, as it's considered a more balanced and polished version of D&D5e by some users.

D&D Tactics?

A common phrase in D&D4e's discussion is that if it had been branded as 'D&D Tactics', it would have seen far better reception. These arguments neglect the system's PR turning on it suddenly before release due to the Open Game License announcements or the fact that it ultimately has the same out- of-combat systems as D&D5e, which is known as a roleplay-heavy system despite absolutely not being one.

The fundamental wisdom behind this description as 'D&D Tactics' is still real in a way, as a major part of D&D4e's design philosophy was in enriching its combat to feel dynamic outside of hitting the opponent and repeating. A large amount of this combat thought and design was later adopted by Lancer and later editions of Pathfinder, such as its second edition. Ultimately, 4e was the source of a large number of successful ideas which existed to make player choices matter more, and it's a shame that players who barely experienced the system give the takeaway that it was 'D&D Tactics', or that WOTC's own takeaway when designing D&D5e was that their mistake was 'making the game elements obvious'.

Balance

D&D4e's balancing is an interesting matter, because the system is widely-considered one of the best-balanced options, with no such thing as a famously-weak class, and the most powerful still only being called 'solid'. Additionally, D&D4e bridged the famous martial-caster divide. There is some controversy over how this was done, as the method was to effectively give martial characters their own 'spells' called exploits, and to limit casters' reality-bending to rituals, which cost gold per use (in-game, not out of game, with the slander 4e has received, I can absolutely see someone willfully misinterpreting that).

Broadly, this universalization of how each class's abilities and power budget was formatted makes for a system that encourages any class to be balanced and viable, although some reasonable counter-arguments are that it was too heavy-handed, and stripped the identities of some classes in the process. In the present, Lancer exists as an important counter-example, which, for all of the inspiration it took from D&D4e, produced a system where character options feel as though they have much more individual personality.

Still, 4e is one of my fundamentally-favourite fantasy RPG systems, as its commitment to balance, especially through all of its shakeups and hurdles is inspiring, and its design, when allowed to be unconstrained, such as psionic powers, druidic wild-shape, or monastic disciplines, all show great potential that was suffocated too early.

Setting

I can't speak much to D&D4e's setting, as it's very similar to the forgotten realms. The Points of Light, 4e's setting, is a broadly-generic fantasy setting designed to be a 'blank slate' for GMs. I'm not the largest fan of this, but it's interesting at least that it carries many of the design decisions of the forgotten realms. This is not a Gorgon, this is a Medusa. A Gorgon in Points of Light is the same bull creature that also can petrify like a Medusa can from the Forgotten Realms. Much of D&D's setting is invented in its old editions, where things were slapped together based on what the writers liked. The reason I feel that newer editions such as D&D4e, D&D5e, and Pathfinder2e simply make their rulesets setting-agnostic and don't change these settings is that the players who care would throw a fit similar in scale to the one which killed D&D4e, and the players who don't care weren't playing in these old settings, anyways.

Class Philosophy

D&D4e's balance comes largely from its class design. All classes fall into one of four roles, those being Defender, Controller, Striker, or Leader. Defenders are slightly more durable, but notably have improved Attacks of Opportunity which can circumvent the usual methods to evade them, and can Mark enemies, forcing those enemies to focus fire on the Defender or suffer a penalty. Controllers are the only role with full and ready access to flexible multi-target attacks and status effects consistently available in their powers, and act as wild cards in their ability to disrupt enemy plans. Strikers have innate DPR steroids, allowing them to output more single-target damage than other classes can by default. Lastly, Leaders all have access to healing abilities inherently.

These roles are balanced between each-other by covering different sections of combat, and then balanced within their own rosters as a way for D&D4e to ensure its class balance. Despite this stringent, system, class individuality still exists, such as Paladins, which are Defenders with access to healing like a miniature Leader. Avengers are Strikers with a focus on hunting a single target who becomes unable to hide, and an inability to change targets until a fight is over or the victim is dead. Rogues are Strikers whose damage steroid is significantly more potent than their contemporaries, but rely on allies helping grant them combat advantage before they can benefit from this steroid. Bards are Leaders who have the ability to Mark enemies on allies' behalf, allowing them to turn an ally into a psuedo-Defender, or even allow a Defender to juggle multiple Marks at once.

Feats, similarly, allow characters to specialize into additional tasks outside of their role's identity, or to double down on their intended roles and strengths, to be significantly more efficient, but limited in scope.
