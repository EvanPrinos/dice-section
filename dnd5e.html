D&D 5th Edition

Easily the largest TTRPG by community and by market presence, Dungeons and Dragons 5th Edition has seen over a decade of popularity. 5e's success as a system is primarily the result of releasing at the time of a cultural resurgence of interest for TTRGPS, however, its own modularity and ease of modification.

5e relies on a fairly light combat style, being tailored toward 'theatre of the mind', a method of combat in which no battlemap is used. While 5e's books encourage this method of play, in actual play many effects will depend on creatures' proximity to one another, and theatre of the mind ends up meaning that the GM is expected to shoulder the burden of keeping track of creatures' positions relative to one-another.

Homebrew

5e's most undeniable strength is its Homebrew. Homebrew is player-made rules or content to modify a TTRPG experience to a table's wants. 5th Edition, due to its popularity and the circumstances surrounding it, has several websites where users can create and even sell their homebrew to people who are interested. Many of these sites even have their own reputations for better or worse.

Bounded Accuracy

A concept that was overall given a name by discourse around D&D 5e, bounded accuracy is the idea that normal rolls don't vary between high and low level characters. It's easier to define Bounded Accuracy by what it isn't, so for context, in D&D 4e, players add half their level to their defenses, attack rolls, and skill checks. The effect of removing this system for D&D 5e was intended to be that enemies weren't locked to characters of specific levels, although generally similar problems could be solved with use of homebrew. Still, it's one of 5e's most thought-out systems, easily.

Balance or Lack Thereof

5th edition's balance has seen heavy criticism over its lifespan, from points about its resource-based classes and its resource-less classes being given the same power baselines, to the reality-bending abilities of magic within its system, while the game seems begrudging to give its martials even basic superhuman feats.

Funnily, one of the largest categories of 5e homebrew is rebalancing the system, either as changes to base 5th edition, or baked into customized rulesets made to tailor its rulesets to other settings (For two such examples, I'd recommend Star Wars 5e and Arknights 5e, which put a lot of effort into trying to solve some such inconsistencies).

Many homebrewers even have a regular phrase between them of 'anything after level 13 doesn't have to be balanced', which stems from the fact that well before that level, most 5e games get so unbalanced that GMs tend to ensure their games end before such levels, so features at that point don't have to be designed with use in mind, so much as to fulfill a class's fantasy.

Setting

5th Edition's setting is the Forgotten Realms, a Tolkien-esque generic fantasy world with a rich cosmology. Since I prefer crafting my own worlds, I don't follow the Forgotten Realms much. What does interest me, though, is how this setting interprets many real-world mythologies into it. Class Philosophy

5th edition's class philosophy is largely nonexistent in terms of thought put into the division of roles and power. The only thing distinguishing the barbarian class as a tank is that it is durable, with zero ways to draw aggro or exceptional ways to punish enemies for ignoring them. Similarly, subclasses cannot consistently distinguish between providing a secondary role or a gameplay gimmick.

Spellcasters 'suffer' especially from this, where their ability to choose spells from their class's entire list, with subclasses only sometimes adding extra spells means that most spellcasters already have access to spells that allow them to cover multiple needs, as well as enough spell slots later on that their 'role' as a resource-based class isn't too valid, either.
