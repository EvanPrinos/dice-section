TTRPGs

A TTRPG, or a Table-Top Role-Playing Game, is by definition a game where players play as characters using a physical medium, traditionally pencils, paper, dice, and possibly figures on a board. By default, most TTRPGs are also characterized by a focus on combat and exploration, as well as teamwork, however theoretically these are not mandatory to the definition.

Additionally, most TTRPGs include a Game Master, or GM, a player whose role is to control the environment and all other characters. This role is traditionally best compared to a game engine, rather than an adversary to the players, as TTRPGs are 'won' by having all players enjoy the session and look forward to playing again and continuing the story or a new one together in the future. Some systems exist that are GM-less, such as using dice rolls and tables to determine how a world would react to the activities of the players' characters, but I have not played such systems yet, so this difference in game design ideology will not be addressed in my current iteration of a Dice-Section.

Characters

Most modern TTRPGs have a focus on allowing players to make characters that are personalized to a fantasy or archetype that the player may want to embody within the world of the game. This often includes stats and abilities, although many systems facilitate this in different ways. A common way in fantasy TTRPGs is classes, which draw from popular fantasy archetypes set out by series such as Lord of the Rings, however classes are not the only axis of character customization, and sometimes aren't one of the axes available at all.

As an enjoyer of High-Fantasy worlds, many of the systems I've played are Fantasy-inspired, however, there will be discussion of games designed to be set in other settings as well, and will highlight some other ways that characters can be customized in further detail in general.

Dice

As mentioned, dice became a traditional staple of TTRPGs early on with the earliest examples, and are broadly used to roll and determine the efficacy of an action taken by one's character. While I lack the omniscience to determine how this decision was made at the time, the core benefit of dice in TTRPGs is to help distinguish between a player and a character. An anecdotal example of this was when my brother was still new to TTRPGs, he would describe his Dungeons and Dragons character delivering fatal blows, and would be upset that the damage dealt didn't reflect the lethality of the attack. The explanation I gave for this disconnect was that the character might not immediately be in an advantageous position to slit an enemy's throat, and the dice help reflect the circumstances surrounding the characters in the game.

Similarly, the dice encourage a level of improvisation to the game which can enliven it. The surface-level example of this is that plans can fail unexpectedly, but more specifically, a failed roll pushes the players to decide why in the story that action failed, such as a moment of distraction, an unfortunate slapstick routine, or whichever other reason pops into the players' heads in the moment. In general, the dice's influence on the game acts as an additional player ensuring that the resulting story is personalized, too, even if the same players play together again.
