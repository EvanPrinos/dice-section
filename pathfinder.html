Pathfinder

Produced as a system after D&D's publishers failed to maintain their magazine contract, Pathfinder the TTRPG was Paizo Publishing's way to stay in business, and these days, Pathfinder second edition, or PF2e, is one of D&D's largest competitors in the fantasy genre. Paizo as a company has generally been one of Pathfinder's greatest assets, as they are known for consistently making user-friendly business decisions.

Among these decisions include heavy player support tools, similar to the ones that D&D has licensed for 5th edition. Pathfinder has Archives of Nethys, an entire maintained wiki which allows a player to access all of PF2e's systems, rules, and definitions for free, as well as Pathbuilder2e, a character creation site which lists all available options, and is a one- time purchase, rather than D&D's subscription-based equivalent.

Homebrew

Pathfinder similarly has a large amount of homebrew available to its players due to its large community, but due to the greater complexity of building a character, many new players may be too dependent on Pathbuilder2e, which doesn't include such content, making homebrew generally less accessible than it is to 5th edition, where D&D's publisher has ensured that its homebrew sites can interface with or are even on the same website as its most popular sponsored character builder.

As a result of this difference in out-of-game infrastructure and my own limited time playing Pathfinder Second Edition, I cannot accurately make statements about the quality of PF2e's homebrew scene.

Feats and Progression

One design field where I think Pathfinder excells is its character progression. Alongside the standard addition of one's level to rolls which is common in many TTRPGs, Pathfinder makes the distinction that even that bonus is still only given to a character with training in whatever they're doing, which heavily encourages cooperation among players to achieve their goals, as a single character very explicitly cannot be so much as passable in every skill check they may need across an adventure. Additionally, with each skill having multiple levels of training it can receive to increase the bonus given, players are allowed to pick up additional skills at later levels or further train their specialties, unlike D&D, which largely suffers from characters having the same skills from start to end of a story, and only really growing in terms of combat ability.

Similarly, PF2e's Feats system is a return to the form of how Feats (or Features) worked in D&D editions before 5e, being an additional trick a character can pick up each level or two, from large list. To discourage optimization, though, PF2e disects feats based on origin, and each point where a character gets to choose a feat, they're told which kind they get to choose, meaning that players don't feel as though they need to prioritize all combat-related feats to stay relevant.

Balance

PF2e's balance due to the aforementioned carefully-engineered progression model is very structurally sound, with characters maintaining similar scopes of ability. Additionally, PF2e curbs the martial-caster divide partly by limiting caster resources. In PF2e, a caster eventually reaches a point where their Spell Slots (the ammunition used to cast spells in most fantasy TTRPGs) stop increasing in number, and instead only grow in power going forward, taking old low-level Spell Slots and turning them into higher-level ones. This serves to keep caster resources much more managable, compared to D&D5e, where a high- level caster can feel inexaustible.

Alongside roping in the higher end of the divide that often faces fantasy TTRPGs, Pathfinder also broadly works to ensure that its martial characters feel as though they have more distinct high points to work with, such as unique class resources to spend, recover in special ways, or above-average power baselines.

Setting

Due to its history of existing due to failed maintenance of a contract, Pathfinder as a franchise of course felt the need to make its own setting for its games to take place in by default. This setting is Golarion, and broadly, it holds many similarities to the Forgotten Realms, such as the same sorts of gorgons that I showcase in the 5th edition page. Whether this was a piece of lore that Paizo wanted to keep, feeling it was iconic, or because they overlooked changing it at all, similarities like this raise intriguing questions, attesting to the law of design "It exists the way it does because somebody made it so".

Class Philosophy

As stated so far, PF2e put a lot of thought into the ecosystem that exists around its classes and characters. Two good examples of similar distinctions it makes to cement class roles are firstly, the limitation of spells through preparation, wherein a character must decide which spells they expect to be using at the start of an adventuring day, and cannot reallocate those spells and spell slots retroactively. The second example is much more sublte, but it's the restriction of the Attack of Opportunity. An Attack of Opportunity in a TTRPG is an attack out of turn in response to an action an enemy took, allowing a character to threaten an enemy to act a certain way, such as discouraging attacking their allies or moving away. In most TTRPGs, Attacks of Opportunity are available to all characters, while in PF2e, they are a class feature or a Feat that must be invested in deliberately later.

Both of these changes serve distinct purposes in encouraging specific teamwork and roles within a party. Being forced to choose one's spells in advance encourages players to divvy up their prepared spells based on coverage, so that the party as a whole doesn't suffer from blind spots, since one character is unable to cover all weaknesses by themselves as a default. Additionally, casters are still given a large list of known spells, encouraging preparing to cover multiple roles, and then deciding with teammates which ones to cover. Similarly, the limitation of Attack of Opportunity to certain characters creates 'anchors' which can reshape the flow of combat around themselves and provide the party a new axis to master.
